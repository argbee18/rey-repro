Kare-Kare

Peanut-braised oxtail & vegetables

A dish of deep roots — slow-cooked oxtail bathed in a rich golden peanut broth, served alongside a sharp bagoong to cut through the velvet. This is Sunday food. This is fiesta food. This is home.

Prep time 30 min
Cook time 3–4 hrs
Serves 6–8
Difficulty Medium
Illustrated in gold & earth
History

Born of a kingdom's table

Kare-Kare is believed to trace its origins to the royal Kapampangan kitchens of Pampanga — a province long regarded as the culinary heartland of the Philippines. Some food historians link it to the Moro soldiers who served the Spanish colonial army, whose curry-like dishes evolved over generations into what we now know.

The name itself is thought to be a reduplication of "kari," the Tamil and Malay word for curry. Yet Kare-Kare bears no spice heat — instead, it is defined by the quiet richness of ground toasted peanuts, thickened with rice flour, fragrant with annatto.

What makes it singular is the pairing: the mild, unctuous stew arrives at the table alongside bagoong alamang — fermented shrimp paste — whose pungent salinity electrifies every bite. Together they are not just complementary; they are necessary to each other.

Today Kare-Kare appears at every fiesta, every family reunion, every occasion that demands a dish cooked with real time and intention. It is patience made edible.

Ingredients

For 6–8 persons
6 servings

The braise

  • Oxtail, cut into segments1.5 kg
  • Tripe (optional)500 g
  • Garlic, crushed8 cloves
  • White onion, quartered1 large
  • Fish sauce (patis)3 tbsp
  • Water or beef stock2.5 L

The peanut sauce

  • Roasted peanuts, ground300 g
  • Peanut butter (unsweetened)4 tbsp
  • Annatto seeds (atsuete)2 tbsp
  • Toasted rice flour3 tbsp
  • Banana blossoms (puso)2 cups

Vegetables & condiment

  • Chinese eggplant3 pcs
  • Long string beans (sitaw)2 bundles
  • Bok choy (pechay)1 head
  • Bagoong alamang (sautéed)½ cup
  • Banana blossom, blanched1 cup

The Method

A recipe that rewards patience. Begin a day before for the deepest flavor, or give it an unhurried afternoon.

01

Blanch the oxtail

Place oxtail (and tripe, if using) in a large pot with cold water. Bring to a rolling boil and cook for 5 minutes. Drain and rinse each piece under cold running water. This removes impurities and ensures a clean, clear broth.

02

Build the broth

Return blanched oxtail to the pot. Add garlic, onion, patis, and enough water or stock to cover by 5 cm. Bring to a boil, skim any foam, then reduce heat to a gentle simmer. Cook low and slow for 2.5 to 3.5 hours, until the meat is tender enough to yield easily to a fork but still holds its shape.

Reserve all the braising liquid — this becomes the base of your sauce. You should have at least 1.5 liters when done.
03

Draw the annatto color

In a small saucepan, warm 3 tablespoons of oil over medium heat. Add annatto seeds and stir gently for 2 minutes — the oil will bloom into a vivid orange-red. Strain out the seeds, discarding them, and reserve the tinted oil. This is what gives Kare-Kare its distinctive golden glow.

04

Sauté the aromatics

In your largest wide pot or kawali, heat the annatto oil over medium-high heat. Sauté onion until translucent, about 3 minutes. Add garlic and cook until fragrant, 1 minute more. Pour in 1.5 liters of the reserved braising liquid and bring to a simmer.

05

Build the peanut sauce

Stir in the ground roasted peanuts and peanut butter. Mix the toasted rice flour with a ladleful of warm broth until smooth, then add to the pot as a thickener. Simmer on low for 15 minutes, stirring often, until the sauce coats the back of a spoon. Season with patis and salt to taste.

Taste as you go: the sauce should be rich and mildly nutty with a savory depth — not sweet. Adjust with more patis if it needs salt or character.
06

Cook the vegetables

Add the braised oxtail to the peanut sauce. In a separate pot of salted boiling water, blanch each vegetable separately: string beans (3 min), eggplant (4 min), pechay (1 min), banana blossom (2 min). Transfer each to ice water immediately to preserve color and texture. Add all blanched vegetables to the stew just before serving — never boil them in the sauce or they will go limp.

07

Serve with bagoong

Ladle generously into wide bowls or serve family-style in the pot. Present alongside warm steamed rice and a ramekin of sautéed bagoong alamang. The bagoong is not optional — it is the counterpoint that makes every spoonful complete.

Sauté the bagoong with garlic, a pinch of sugar, and a splash of vinegar 5 minutes before serving for the best result.

Before you begin — a few notes from the kitchen

Toast your own peanuts

Pre-ground peanuts from a jar lack the depth of freshly toasted ones. Roast raw peanuts in a dry pan until golden-brown and fragrant, cool completely, then pulse in a blender until the texture of fine sand. The difference is significant.

Low and slow wins

Rushing the braise produces tough, chewy oxtail. A pressure cooker can shorten the process to 45 minutes, but a traditional pot — given 3 unrushed hours — produces a silkier, more deeply flavored result. If you have time, use it.

The rice flour secret

Toast raw rice in a dry pan until it turns light golden, then grind. This thickener adds a distinct nuttiness that cornstarch cannot replicate. Mix it with cold broth before adding to the hot pot to avoid lumps.

Bagoong makes the dish

Do not serve Kare-Kare without bagoong. The stew's richness needs the fermented shrimp paste's saltiness and funk to come alive. Sauté it briefly with garlic before serving — raw bagoong is too sharp.

It improves overnight

Like most braises, Kare-Kare is better the next day. The peanut sauce thickens and deepens in flavor after resting in the refrigerator. Add the vegetables fresh when reheating — never store them in the sauce overnight.

Vegetable timing matters

Always blanch vegetables separately and briefly. Bok choy wilts in under a minute; eggplant needs 3–4. Adding them to the simmering sauce instead will turn them into mush. Blanch, shock in ice water, add at the last moment.
